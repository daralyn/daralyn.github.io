Geneva Mechanism

May 2018

Quick Overview

For this project, we looked at videos showing how the Geneva Mechanism moves/works. Then we had to design one ourselves and think about how it moves and think about clearances between parts. Larger clearances require less design accuracy but also can cause it not to operate correctly. Having no clearances would not allow it to move. We used the laser cutter to cut the pieces out and test if the clearances worked. I made adjustments, and then we used Fusion 360's CAM software to generate code to cut out the drive wheel. We then cut out the remaining pieces on the water jet, and assembled it.

What I learned...

In this short project, I learned about how important clearance is. I also was refreshed on how many of the machines worked. We used the HAAS CNC mill to cut out the drive wheel, and used the water jet to cut out the base plate, driven member and bottom piece. Then to cut the steel shafts, we used the horizontal band saw.
While designing the piece and prototyping it with plywood using the laser cutter, it was important to think about clearances and heights. I thought it was especially interesting to press fit the shafts into the pieces, using the arbor press, and also to drill holes in the base plates that would be perfect fits.

What was difficult...

I think the most difficult part was getting started and designing the pieces to fit properly together. I wanted to try to understand how it fit and worked, but it was really hard, so I used a drawing from the internet to design the part. I took their measurements, and tweaked them a little bit after seeing how well they fit together, and seeing if I needed more clearance. I also, after I had assembled it, I thought it was difficult to figure out what was making the driven member keep tilting upward on one side only.

If I were to do it again...

If I were to do it again, I would have liked to have more time. I think figuring out how to design it without drawings would also be really cool and valuable to better understand how the parts fit. I think trying it out and testing to see if it fits and learning from those errors is the best way for me to learn and remember things.
